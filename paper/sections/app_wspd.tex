\section{The local WSPD death-time implementation}\label{sec:wspd}

This appendix discharges the one implementation step deferred by \cref{sec:dt}: realizing a locally
navigable Euclidean $(1+\rho)$-spanner of a point set $X\subseteq[\Delta]^2$ under the range-counting
oracle, and running the clipped death-time sampler of \cref{sec:dt-sampler} on it. We build on the
well-separated pair decomposition (WSPD) construction of Driemel et al.~\cite{DMOSW25} (whose spanner
facts are from Har-Peled~\cite{HarPeled11} and whose sampling primitive is from
Monemizadeh~\cite{Monemizadeh23}). We use \emph{cell} for a node of the quadtree $\mathcal T$ on
$[\Delta]^2$; a cell $c$ has side length $r(c)$ and diameter $\sqrt2\,r(c)$, and $d(c,c')$ is the distance
between cell centers. The vertex set is $X$; for $X=P$ a cell is nonempty when $\rcount\cdot>0$, and for a
snapped support $X=Q$ an $h$-cell is nonempty exactly when the aligned $P$-region is nonempty, so every
emptiness test is one count on $P$ (\cref{sec:dt}). Throughout, the WSPD separation parameter is a fixed
$\Theta(\rho)$, chosen so that the spanner stretch below is $1+\rho$.

\subsection{Uniform point sampling under the oracle}\label{sec:prelim-sample}

We first record the sampling primitive used here and in \cref{sec:support} (referenced from
\cref{sec:support-oracle}). It is \emph{telescoping sampling}~\cite{Monemizadeh23}.

\begin{lemma}[Uniform sampling]\label{lem:telescoping}
Given a query region $R$ that is a union of quadtree cells with $\rcount R>0$, one can return a point of
$P\cap R$ drawn uniformly at random using $O(\log\Delta)$ range-counting queries.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $R$ is a union of several cells, first pick one of them with probability proportional to its count
(one count per cell). Then walk down $\mathcal T$ from the chosen cell: at a cell $c$ with children
$c_1,\dots,c_4$, query the four counts $\rcount{c_i}$ (which sum to $\rcount c$) and descend into $c_i$
with probability $\rcount{c_i}/\rcount c$. After $O(\log\Delta)$ levels the walk reaches a single grid
point, uniform on $P\cap R$ by the chain of conditional probabilities. Each level spends $O(1)$ counts.
\end{proof}

Sampling a uniform \emph{vertex} of $X$ has two costs. For $X=P$ a uniform vertex is a uniform point,
$O(\log\Delta)$ counts. For a support $X=Q$ a uniform support cell is drawn by rejection
(\cref{sec:support-oracle}): sample a uniform point of $P$, accept its $h$-cell with probability
$1/\abs{P\cap C}$, which accepts with probability $K/n$ and so costs $\Otil(n/K)$ counts per support
vertex. We use the first in the bulk sampler for $P$ and the second in the sampler for $Q$.

\subsection{The spanner realized by range counts}\label{sec:wspd-spanner}

\paragraph{Representatives.}
For a nonempty cell $c$, its representative $\rep(c)$ is the vertex of $X$ inside $c$ that is first in the
$z$-order (the order a quadtree traversal visits leaves). For $X=P$ it is found by descending $\mathcal T$
from $c$, at each level entering the $z$-first nonempty child (one emptiness count per child), reaching a
single point in $O(\log\Delta)$ counts; for $X=Q$ the descent is over $h$-cells and returns the $z$-first
nonempty $h$-cell center, again $O(\log\Delta)$ counts. The $z$-order is a total order, so $\rep(c)$ is
unique and every search returns the same representative for the same cell; this fixes all ties. The
computation returns the \emph{coordinates} of $\rep(c)$, so exact Euclidean distances between
representatives are available with no further queries.

\paragraph{Well-separated pairs.}
A pair of cells $(c,c')$ is \emph{well-separated} if the separation parameter times $\max\{r(c),r(c')\}$
is at most $d(c,c')$. The WSPD $\mathcal W$ of $X$ is the family produced by the Callahan--Kosaraju
recursion~\cite{CK95} on $\mathcal T$. We never materialize $\mathcal W$; two facts let us use it locally.
\begin{enumerate}
\item Membership $(c,c')\in\mathcal W$ is decided in $O(1)$ counts. Well-separation is a geometric test on
the two cells (no query). The recursion (which always splits the cell of larger side) places $(c,c')$ in
$\mathcal W$ exactly when both cells are nonempty, $(c,c')$ is well-separated, and the \emph{oriented
parent} pair, obtained by replacing the larger cell with its quadtree parent, is not well-separated;
the two emptiness tests are $O(1)$ counts.
\item (\cite{HarPeled11}, restated in \cite{DMOSW25}.) For every $(c,c')\in\mathcal W$, $r(c')/2\le
r(c)\le 2r(c')$, and after redundant-pair removal (below) every point of $[\Delta]^2$ lies in
$O(\rho^{-2}\log\Delta)$ pairs of $\mathcal W$, which number $O(\rho^{-2}n)$ in total.
\end{enumerate}
The \emph{spanner} $S=(X,E(S))$ has, for each $(c,c')\in\mathcal W$, the edge $\{\rep(c),\rep(c')\}$ whose
weight is the \emph{exact Euclidean length} $\norm{\rep(c)-\rep(c')}$. Every edge of $S$ is therefore a
genuine pair of points of $X$ carrying its true distance, so $S$ is a Euclidean subgraph of the complete
graph on $X$; this is what \cref{sec:dt} needs.

\paragraph{Redundant-pair suppression.}
Running the recursion on the quadtree of $[\Delta]^2$ (not on the points) can emit two pairs $(c,c')$ and
$(\bar c,\bar c')$ that carry the same point sets, which happens only for \emph{nested} cells: $\bar
c\subseteq c$, $\bar c'\subseteq c'$ with $P\cap c=P\cap\bar c$ and $P\cap c'=P\cap\bar c'$. For nested
cells, equal counts ($\rcount c=\rcount{\bar c}$, one count each) imply equal point sets, which is the
test we use. Because the edge weight is the exact Euclidean distance between representatives, and equal
point sets give the same $z$-first representative, all such duplicates produce the \emph{identical} edge
$\{\rep(c),\rep(c')\}$ of the same weight. Suppressing them therefore does not change the edge set $E(S)$;
it only removes repeated work, and is what makes fact~(2)'s $O(\rho^{-2}\log\Delta)$ count of
\emph{distinct} incident edges per vertex hold.

\paragraph{Stretch.}
The spanner inherits the standard WSPD guarantee at separation $\Theta(\rho)$.

\begin{lemma}[Spanner stretch \cite{HarPeled11,DMOSW25}]\label{lem:stretch}
$S$ is a $(1+\rho)$-spanner of $X$: for all $x,y\in X$ there is an $x$--$y$ path in $S$ of length at most
$(1+\rho)\norm{x-y}$. Consequently $\wt(\MST(S))\le(1+\rho)\,\wt(\MST(X))$, and for every threshold $t$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:appA-stretch}
  c_X(t)\le c_S(t)\le c_X\!\bigl(t/(1+\rho)\bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The spanner property is the WSPD spanner theorem~\cite[Thm.~on WSPD spanners]{HarPeled11} with separation
$\Theta(\rho)$; the MST bound is its Lemma~23 in \cite{DMOSW25}. For \eqref{eq:appA-stretch}: every
$S$-edge is a true $X$-pair, so the graph of $S$-edges of length $\le t$ is a subgraph of the $X$-pairs of
length $\le t$, giving $c_X(t)\le c_S(t)$. Conversely, if $\norm{x-y}\le t/(1+\rho)$ then the spanner path
between $x,y$ has every edge of length $\le(1+\rho)\norm{x-y}\le t$, so $x,y$ lie in one component of the
$S$-edges of length $\le t$; hence $c_S(t)\le c_X(t/(1+\rho))$.
\end{proof}

Integrating \eqref{eq:appA-stretch} against \eqref{eq:cluster-integral} yields the additive spanner bias
$A_L(X)\le A_L(H_X)\le(1+\rho)A_L(X)$ of \eqref{eq:spanner-stretch}.

\subsection{Local navigability}\label{sec:wspd-nav}

The death-time search needs, from a vertex of $S$, its incident spanner edges within a given length. We
build this from the cell-level neighbor oracle of \cite{DMOSW25}.

\begin{lemma}[Cell-neighbor oracle, {\cite[Lemma~24]{DMOSW25}}]\label{lem:navigate}
Let $c$ be a cell of side $r/2$. All cells $c'$ of side $r/2$ for which some WSPD pair links a subcell of
$c$ to a subcell of $c'$ at separation distance realizing an $S$-edge of length at most $r$ can be found
using $O(\rho^{-2})$ range-counting queries.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is Lemma~24 of \cite{DMOSW25}, stated there with the WSPD separation written as $\eps$; we use
$\Theta(\rho)$. If an $S$-edge of length $\le r$ links $c$ and $c'$ then $d(c,c')\le 3r$, and there are
$O(1)$ cells $c'$ of side $r/2$ with $d(c,c')\le 3r$. For each, the existence of a linking WSPD pair is
decided by a witness search truncated at side $2^{i^*}$ with $2^{i^*}\le\rho r/10<2^{i^*+1}$: a witness is
a pair $(\bar c,\bar c')$ of nonempty cells of side $\ge 2^{i^*}$, $\bar c\subseteq c$, $\bar
c'\subseteq c'$, with either $(\bar c,\bar c')\in\mathcal W$ and $d(\bar c,\bar c')\le r$, or $(\bar
c,\bar c')$ not well-separated and $d(\bar c,\bar c')+r(\bar c)+r(\bar c')\le r$. There are
$O(\rho^{-2})$ subcells of side $\ge 2^{i^*}$ inside $c$, hence $O(\rho^{-4})$ candidate witness pairs;
a modification of the recursion that tests membership and emptiness ($O(1)$ counts each) and prunes
well-separated branches early completes the search in $O(\rho^{-2})$ counts. Correctness of the
truncation is the argument of \cite[Lemma~24]{DMOSW25}: a too-small defining pair forces its parent pair
to be non-well-separated, bringing the endpoints within $2r/5$ and so producing a side-$2^{i^*}$ witness.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Vertex incident-edge oracle]\label{lem:incident}
For a vertex $u\in X$ and a length $r$, all $S$-edges incident to $u$ of length at most $r$, together with
their exact weights, can be enumerated using $O(\rho^{-2}\log^2\Delta)$ range-counting queries; their
number is $O(\rho^{-2}\log\Delta)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every incident edge $\{u,u'\}$ comes from a pair $(c,c')\in\mathcal W$ with $u\in\{\rep(c),\rep(c')\}$;
say $\rep(c)=u$. Then $u\in c$, so $c$ is one of the $O(\log\Delta)$ quadtree ancestors of the leaf of
$u$, and $u$ is the $z$-first vertex in $c$. We iterate over those ancestors: for each ancestor $c$ with
$\rep(c)=u$ (verified by one representative computation, $O(\log\Delta)$ counts), apply
\cref{lem:navigate} at the scale matching $r(c)$ to obtain the $O(1)$ candidate neighbor cells $c'$
($O(\rho^{-2})$ counts), test $(c,c')\in\mathcal W$ ($O(1)$ counts), compute $\rep(c')$
($O(\log\Delta)$ counts), and emit $\{u,\rep(c')\}$ with weight $\norm{u-\rep(c')}$ if this is at most
$r$. Summing $O(\rho^{-2})+O(\log\Delta)$ per ancestor over $O(\log\Delta)$ ancestors gives
$O(\rho^{-2}\log^2\Delta)$ counts. Distinctness and the $O(\rho^{-2}\log\Delta)$ bound on the number of
incident edges follow from fact~(2) of \cref{sec:wspd-spanner} after redundant-pair suppression.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The exact bottleneck death-time search}\label{sec:wspd-search}

We now compute the clipped death time $X_L(v)=\min\{\tau(v),L\}$ of a seed $v$ exactly. Fix once a
\emph{root} $r_X=\rep(\text{root cell of }\mathcal T)$, the global $z$-first vertex of $X$, and assign it
rank $-\infty$; assign every other vertex an independent uniform rank in $[0,1]$, drawn lazily and keyed
by \emph{vertex identity} (the point coordinates for $X=P$, the $h$-cell identifier for $X=Q$), so a
vertex has one rank no matter through which cell it is reached, and ranks are a.s.\ distinct (ties have
probability zero and are broken by $z$-order). As in \cref{sec:dt-sampler}, $\tau(v)$ is the bottleneck
(min--max-edge) distance from $v$ to the root or to any lower-ranked vertex.

\paragraph{The search.}
Run a bottleneck variant of Dijkstra from $v$: maintain, for each reached vertex, the smallest value
$\beta(\cdot)$ such that $v$ reaches it by a path whose every edge is $\le\beta(\cdot)$, in a priority
queue keyed by $\beta$ over \emph{exact} edge weights. Repeatedly extract the queue-minimum vertex $u$;
if $u=r_X$ or $\mathrm{rank}(u)<\mathrm{rank}(v)$, output $\tau(v)=\beta(u)$ and stop. Otherwise expand
$u$ by \cref{lem:incident} at length $\min\{\beta(u)\cdot(1+\rho),L\}$ (enlarging the explored length only
as the frontier grows), relaxing each incident edge $\{u,u'\}$ of weight $\omega$ to
$\beta(u')\leftarrow\min\{\beta(u'),\max\{\beta(u),\omega\}\}$. If the queue-minimum $\beta$ exceeds $L$
before any halt, output $X_L(v)=L$. Because the queue extracts vertices in increasing $\beta$ and $\beta$
is computed from exact weights, the value at the first extraction of a root/lower-ranked vertex is exactly
$\tau(v)$, and the $L$-cutoff returns exactly $\min\{\tau(v),L\}$; no rounding enters.

\paragraph{Per-search cost in expectation.}
Order the vertices by bottleneck distance from $v$. The search halts at the first root/lower-ranked vertex
in this order, so it extracts the $j$-th vertex only if none of the first $j-1$ is the root and $v$ has
smaller rank than all of them, an event of probability $\le 1/(j-1)$ for $j\ge2$ (the ranks of the first
$j-1$ vertices and $v$ are exchangeable). The expected number of extractions is thus at most
$1+\sum_{j\ge2}1/(j-1)=1+H_{\abs V-1}=O(\log\abs V)$. Each extraction expands one vertex by
\cref{lem:incident} at $O(\rho^{-2}\log^2\Delta)$ counts, so a search costs
$O(\rho^{-2}\log^2\Delta)\cdot O(\log\abs V)=\poly(\rho^{-1})\poly\log(n,\Delta)$ range counts in
expectation, independent of $n^{1/3}$, i.e.\ $\Otil_\rho(1)$.

\subsection{The global query budget}\label{sec:wspd-cap}

We do \emph{not} cap individual searches or impute a value to an unfinished one, since imputing $X_L=L$
would bias the death-time mean upward when $\tau(v)<L$. Instead we bound the whole estimator call.
The bulk sampler for $A_L(P)$ runs $s_P=O(\xi^{-2}n^{1/3})$ searches, each $\Otil_\rho(1)$ counts in
expectation (seeds drawn at $O(\log\Delta)$ counts by \cref{lem:telescoping}); the sampler for $A_L(Q)$
runs $s_Q=O_\xi(1)$ searches with seeds at $\Otil(n/K)=\Otil(n^{1/3})$ counts each. Hence the call has
expected query cost
\begin{equation}\label{eq:appA-totalcost}
  \overline Q=n^{1/3}\cdot\poly(\rho^{-1},\xi^{-1})\cdot\poly\log(n,\Delta)=\Otil_\eps(n^{1/3}).
\end{equation}
Fix a deterministic budget $B=C\overline Q$ for an absolute constant $C$ and abort the call, returning
\textsc{fail}, the moment the running count exceeds $B$. By Markov's inequality the call exceeds $B$ with
probability at most $1/C$, a small constant, and \textsc{fail} is treated by the geometric $W$-search of
\cref{sec:main-combine} as a non-stopping outcome inside its constant-probability amplification. A call
that does not abort returns the unbiased clipped death-time averages of \cref{sec:dt-sampler}. The cost is
$B=\Otil_\eps(n^{1/3})$ deterministically, and the displayed $\poly(1/\eps)$ factors in
\eqref{eq:appA-totalcost} are hidden by $\Otil_\eps$ for fixed $\eps$; for variable $\eps$ the bound reads
$n^{1/3}\poly(1/\eps)\poly\log(n,\Delta)$.
