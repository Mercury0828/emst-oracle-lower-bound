\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

The weight of a Euclidean minimum spanning tree is one of the oldest statistics of a point set, and
estimating it without reading the whole input is a recurring sublinear-algorithms question. It is a
natural proxy for how spread out a point set is, it controls the cost of near-neighbor and clustering
structures built on the points, and it is the planar instance of a family of geometric cost functionals
(MST, Steiner tree, matching, transportation) that one would like to estimate from compact summaries of a
massive spatial dataset. For $n$ points in the plane the tree itself has $n-1$ edges, but its total length
is a single number, and a long line of work asks how cheaply that number can be approximated. The answer
depends entirely on how the algorithm is allowed to touch the points: the weaker the access primitive, the
more the algorithm must infer indirectly, and the more interesting the query complexity becomes. Chazelle, Rubinfeld, and Trevisan~\cite{CRT05} showed that for a graph given
by adjacency lists the MST weight is $(1\pm\eps)$-estimable in time independent of the number of vertices,
and Czumaj, Sohler, and coauthors~\cite{Czumaj05,CzumajSohler09} carried the program into geometry, where
the cost is measured in queries to a geometric oracle.

We work in the \emph{orthogonal range-counting} model studied by Driemel, Monemizadeh, Oh, Staals, and
Woodruff~\cite{DMOSW25}: the point set $P\subseteq[\Delta]^2$ (with $\Delta=O(n)$) is accessed only
through a data structure that, given an axis-aligned rectangle $R$, returns $\abs{P\cap R}$, and the cost
of an algorithm is the number of such queries. This is the native primitive of a spatial database, and it
is strictly weaker than the distance and nearest-neighbor oracles used in earlier geometric MST work: a
range count reveals how many points fall in a box but neither which points nor where. Driemel et
al.~\cite{DMOSW25} gave an $\Otil(\sqrt n)$-query algorithm in this model together with an
$\Omega(n^{1/3})$-query lower bound, and left the polynomial gap between the two as the open question. We
close it.

\subsection{Our results}\label{sec:intro-results}

Our main result is a range-counting algorithm whose query cost matches the lower bound up to
polylogarithmic and $\eps$-dependent factors.

\begin{theorem*}[\cref{thm:main}]
For every fixed $\eps\in(0,1)$ and every $P\subseteq[\Delta]^2$ with $\abs P=n$ and $\Delta=O(n)$, there
is a randomized algorithm that, with probability at least $2/3$, returns $\widehat W$ with
$(1-\eps)\,\wt(\MST(P))\le\widehat W\le(1+\eps)\,\wt(\MST(P))$ using $\Otil_\eps(n^{1/3})$ orthogonal
range-counting queries.
\end{theorem*}

The success probability is boosted to $1-\delta$ by $O(\log(1/\delta))$ independent repetitions and a
median. Since Driemel et al.~\cite{DMOSW25} prove that $\Omega(n^{1/3})$ queries are necessary even to
estimate $\wt(\MST(P))$ within a constant factor, \cref{thm:main} settles the polynomial query exponent
of Euclidean MST-weight estimation in this model, the two bounds agreeing up to polylogarithmic and
$\eps$-dependent factors (\cref{sec:optimality}). \Cref{tab:prior} places the bound in context.

The engine behind \cref{thm:main} is a self-contained estimator for the MST weight of a grid
\emph{support}, which we isolate because it is where the improvement lives.

\begin{lemma*}[\cref{lem:support}]
For every $\eta\in(0,\tfrac14)$ there is a randomized algorithm that, with probability at least $2/3$,
estimates the MST weight $\Wq$ of the set $Q$ of nonempty-cell centers of a grid imposed on $P$ to within
a $(1\pm\eta)$ factor, using $\Otil_\eta(\sqrt K+n/K)$ range-counting queries, where $K=\abs Q$.
\end{lemma*}

Balancing the two terms of \cref{lem:support} at $K=\Theta(n^{2/3})$ gives the $n^{1/3}$ rate; the
reduction from $P$ to a support of this size, and the routing of the heavy tail of long MST edges, is the
content of \cref{sec:dt,sec:main}. Together with the lower bound of~\cite{DMOSW25}, \cref{thm:main} pins
the exponent exactly.

\begin{corollary*}[\cref{cor:tight}]
For every fixed $\eps\in(0,1)$, the randomized orthogonal range-counting query complexity of
$(1\pm\eps)$-estimating the planar Euclidean MST weight of $n$ points in $[\Delta]^2$ with $\Delta=O(n)$ is
$n^{1/3}$, up to polylogarithmic and $\eps$-dependent factors.
\end{corollary*}

We make three remarks on the scope. First, the result is stated for the plane, where the balance point
$K=\Theta(n^{2/3})$ and the packing exponent live; the cluster-count integral and the spanner construction
themselves extend to any fixed dimension. Second, the algorithm is randomized
Monte Carlo with constant success probability, amplified by repetition and a median as usual. Third, the
running time on a word RAM matches the query bound up to polylogarithmic factors (\cref{rem:time}), so the
result is a sublinear-time \emph{algorithm}, not only a query bound.

\subsection{Techniques}\label{sec:intro-tech}

\paragraph{The barrier, and sampling space instead of mass.}
By the Chazelle--Rubinfeld--Trevisan identity~\cite{CRT05}, the MST weight is the area under the
cluster-count curve, $\wt(\MST(S))=\int_0^\infty(c_S(t)-1)\,dt$, where $c_S(t)$ counts the single-linkage
clusters of $S$ at scale $t$ (\cref{sec:prelim}). Estimating the curve by sampling \emph{points} and
exploring their neighborhoods is the natural approach, and it is exactly what costs $\Omega(\sqrt n)$: a
few far-flung points can carry a constant fraction of the weight while occupying a vanishing fraction of
the mass, so a mass sample misses them and undercounts. The one new primitive of the paper samples
\emph{space} instead of mass. At each scale we draw candidate grid cells uniformly from a universe that
\emph{includes empty cells} and, on an occupied cell, run a min-rank exploration that returns a nonzero
value only at the unique \emph{leader} of its component. This \emph{empty-cell spatial leader estimator}
(\cref{sec:support-estimator}) has expectation equal to the component count $c$ and variance at most $Mc$,
with $M$ the universe size, so its cost is governed by the number of components and not by the number of
occupied cells, and a heavy outlier is sampled with the right probability regardless of its tiny mass.

This estimator drives a support-MST routine (\cref{lem:support}); the reduction from $P$ to a grid support
of size $\Theta(n^{2/3})$ uses a clipped death-time sampler for the bulk of $P$, support regularization
and snapping for the tail, and an assembly $\widehat W=\widehat A_P+\widehat\Wq-\widehat A_Q$ with a
geometric search for the scale of $W$. The death-time sampler runs on a locally navigable
$(1+\rho)$-spanner realized under the oracle by a well-separated pair decomposition (WSPD)~\cite{CK95,HarPeled11}.
\Cref{sec:overview} develops all of this as a connected story; the formal statements and proofs follow in
\cref{sec:support,sec:dt,sec:main} and the appendices.

\subsection{Related work}\label{sec:intro-related}

\paragraph{Sublinear MST weight.}
The graph model was opened by Chazelle, Rubinfeld, and Trevisan~\cite{CRT05}, who observed that the MST
weight is determined by the connected-component counts of the threshold graphs and that these counts can
be estimated by sampling vertices and exploring bounded portions of their components, yielding a
$(1\pm\eps)$-estimator in time $\Otil(d_{\max}\eps^{-2})$ for graphs of average degree $d_{\max}$,
independent of the number of vertices. This component-counting reduction, and the cluster-count integral
$\wt(\MST)=\int_0^\infty(c-1)$ it rests on, is the backbone of every result in the area, ours included.
Czumaj and Sohler~\cite{CzumajSohler09,CzumajSohler04} carried it to general metrics under a distance
oracle, where the same $\Otil(\poly(1/\eps))$-time bound holds; Patlin and van den Brand~\cite{PatlinvdB25}
recently revisited the running-time dependence with a simpler analysis. What changes from one model to the
next is not the reduction but the cost of estimating one component count, and that single subroutine is
where the geometry, and our improvement, lives.

\paragraph{Euclidean MST estimation and geometric oracles.}
Computing the Euclidean MST exactly is classical: Yao~\cite{Yao82} gave the first subquadratic algorithms
in fixed dimension, Agarwal, Edelsbrunner, Schwarzkopf, and Welzl~\cite{AESW91} tied it to bichromatic
closest pairs, and fast practical variants are widely used~\cite{MarchRamGray10}. The sublinear
\emph{estimation} of its weight begins with Czumaj, Erg\"un, Fortnow, Magen, Newman, Rubinfeld, and
Sohler~\cite{Czumaj05}, who gave the first sublinear planar estimator, a $(1\pm\eps)$-approximation in
$\Otil_\eps(\sqrt n)$ time, using range-emptiness queries together with approximate cone nearest-neighbor
search; the same group's metric reduction~\cite{CzumajSohler04,CzumajSohler09} and the early sublinear
geometric algorithms of Chazelle, Liu, and Magen~\cite{ChazelleLiuMagen03} set the stage. In general metrics given by a distance
matrix, Chen, Khanna, and Tan~\cite{ChenKhannaTan23} settle the space/query trade-off for estimating MST
and TSP cost; these bounds are for the distance-matrix oracle and do not transfer to range counting,
where distances are never revealed. Arya, Mount, and
Park~\cite{AryaMountPark15} studied geometric MST range queries with approximate-range data structures,
building on approximate range searching~\cite{AryaMount00}, and range-clustering and approximate-range
clustering queries were studied by Abrahamsen et al.~\cite{AbrahamsenEtal17} and Oh and
Ahn~\cite{OhAhn18}. The range-counting oracle of Driemel et al.~\cite{DMOSW25} is the setting of the
present paper; their $\Otil(\sqrt n)$ upper bound also reduces to component counting, via the
Callahan--Kosaraju WSPD spanner~\cite{CK95,HarPeled11,NarasimhanSmid07}, but pays $\sqrt n$ at the
component-counting step, which is the cost our spatial leader estimator removes. The Euclidean spanners we
rely on descend from a long line~\cite{Vaidya91,ADMSS95}, surveyed in~\cite{BoseSmid13,NarasimhanSmid07}.
The oracle has since been applied to convex-hull and emptiness problems~\cite{SchiblerXueZhu26}. Orthogonal
range searching is itself a heavily studied data-structuring primitive, in both the reporting/counting
data structures~\cite{Bentley75,Willard85,Chazelle90,ChanPatrascu10,JaJaMortensenShi04,
AfshaniArgeLarsen09,ChanLarsenPatrascu11,ChanTsakalidis18,Chan19,ShengTao11} and the surveys and
textbooks~\cite{Matousek94,AgarwalErickson99,deBergEtal08}.

\paragraph{Single-linkage clustering and related primitives.}
The components of the threshold graph are the single-linkage clusters, and the cost of single-linkage
clustering has been studied directly in the sublinear regime by Peng, Sohler, and
Xu~\cite{PSX25}; MST and clustering under strong and weak distance oracles were studied
in~\cite{MetricOracleMST23}, and the query complexity of the related metric Steiner tree problem by Chen
and Khanna~\cite{ChenKhanna23Steiner}. Streaming Euclidean MST to a constant factor was given by Chen, Cohen-Addad,
Jayaram, Levi, and Waingarten~\cite{ChenCAJLW23}; the contraction trick that keeps component counts
stable under additive error, which we reuse in the support regularization, traces to dynamic-stream sampling
of Frahling, Indyk, and Sohler~\cite{FrahlingIndykSohler05} and Indyk~\cite{Indyk04} and to
relative-error $L_p$-sampling~\cite{MonemizadehWoodruff10}. Estimating the number of connected components in sublinear time, the
abstraction at the heart of all these reductions, was isolated by Berenbrink, Krayenhoff, and
Mallmann-Trenn~\cite{BKM14} building on the bounded-degree property testing of Goldreich and
Ron~\cite{GoldreichRon02} and the metric sublinear algorithms of Indyk~\cite{Indyk99}. Component counting
and related sublinear estimators are part of the broader property-testing toolkit~\cite{CzumajSohler01,
CzumajSohler10,EdenRonSeshadhri18,OnakEtal12,RubinfeldShapira11,BehnezhadRoghaniRubinstein23,CormodeEtal17},
alongside sublinear algorithms for sequence and string problems~\cite{GoldenbergKrauthgamerSaha19,
KociumakaSaha20,MitzenmacherSeddighin21} and for learning geometric
shapes~\cite{HarPeledJonesRahul21}, with concentration via Chernoff bounds~\cite{Chernoff52}. The WSPD and geometric spanners are standard tools
(Callahan--Kosaraju~\cite{CK95}, Har-Peled~\cite{HarPeled11}, Narasimhan--Smid~\cite{NarasimhanSmid07}),
and telescoping range-count sampling is due to Monemizadeh~\cite{Monemizadeh23}. The drive to compute
structural quantities under a constrained access budget recurs well beyond the sublinear-query setting
studied here: in communication-efficient distributed computation~\cite{Zhong25UNIQ}, in privacy-preserving
distributed computation~\cite{Zhong24DP}, in graph-learning models for large interconnected
networks~\cite{Shen26CBAM}, and in distributionally robust optimization for networked
systems~\cite{Shen26Bilevel,Shen25JointBidding}.

The paper is organized as the techniques above suggest: \cref{sec:prelim} fixes the model and the
cluster-count integral; \cref{sec:support} states and uses the support-MST estimator; \cref{sec:dt}
gives the death-time primitive and the reduction for $P$; \cref{sec:main} assembles the main theorem;
\cref{sec:optimality} records the match to the lower bound; and \cref{sec:open} collects open questions.
Full proofs are in \cref{sec:wspd,sec:app-lemma3,sec:app-assembly}.
