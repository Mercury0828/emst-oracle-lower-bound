\section{Open questions}\label{sec:open}

\paragraph{Earth Mover's Distance.}
The Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) between two planar point sets sits in the same range-counting framework of
Driemel et al.~\cite{DMOSW25} as MST weight. Their EMD results give an $O(\log\Delta)$ multiplicative
approximation with an additive-error trade-off that is tight in its own regime; whether a near-$(1\pm\eps)$
estimate of planar EMD is achievable under a pure range-counting oracle, and at what query rate, is open.
Sublinear and sketching algorithms for EMD have a long
history~\cite{AndoniDoBaIndykWoodruff09,DoBaEtal11,Charikar02,AndoniIndykKrauthgamer08}, as do near-linear geometric matching and
transportation algorithms~\cite{AgarwalChangRaghvendraXiao22,AgarwalChangXiao19,SharathkumarAgarwal12,
AgarwalSharathkumar14,KhesinNikolovParamonov19}, but none operates under a pure range-counting oracle.

EMD has a dyadic structure parallel to the cluster-count integral: writing the two point sets as red and
blue of equal mass, an optimal transport is approximated within $O(\log\Delta)$ (the multiplicative loss
already present in~\cite{DMOSW25}) by the quadtree sum that charges, per cell and dyadic level, the
absolute red-minus-blue imbalance times the cell diameter. The analogue of estimating $c_S(t)$ is then
estimating the per-scale \emph{total imbalance}. Our leader estimator is not specific to component
counting, so two ingredients, which we state as open problems and not claims, would carry it over. First, a
\emph{spatial estimator} for the absolute imbalance $\sum_{\text{cells}}\abs{\text{red}-\text{blue}}$ (the
signed counts cancel over a level, so $\abs{\cdot}$ is what one wants): draw a candidate cell uniformly and
return its $\abs{\text{red}-\text{blue}}$ scaled by the universe size; whether its variance admits a
control as clean as the leader estimator's $Mc$ bound is open. Second, a \emph{packing inequality} relating
the imbalance profile to the EMD, in the role of $\Wq\ge b\delta/16$. We conjecture that these, with the
same $\sqrt K+n/K$ balance, would estimate the dyadic transport approximation in $\Otil(n^{1/3})$ queries,
hence $O(\log\Delta)$-approximate EMD at that rate; whether the spatial-sampling idea reaches the true
matching cost, and what the right
range-counting lower bound for EMD is, are the deeper questions.

\paragraph{Higher dimensions.}
For $P\subseteq[\Delta]^d$ with $d\ge3$ the WSPD spanner and the cluster-count integral both go through,
but the packing exponent and the balance point $K=\Theta(n^{2/3})$ are tuned to the plane. The
dimension-dependence of the optimal range-counting exponent, and whether it degrades gracefully with $d$,
is open.

\paragraph{The cost of learning $K$.}
The support estimator pays $\Otil(\sqrt K+n/K)$, and the additive $n/K$ is purely the cost of estimating
the support size $K$ by rejection sampling, which is what forces the balance at $K=\Theta(n^{2/3})$. If $K$
were known, or estimable more cheaply, the support estimator would cost $\Otil(\sqrt K)$ alone, and the
reduction could in principle use a smaller support. Whether the $n/K$ term is necessary, or whether the
support size can be learned in $o(n/K)$ queries (perhaps amortized across the geometric search, which
already touches the support at many scales), is open, and a positive answer might lower the exponent below
$1/3$ for inputs whose support is easy to count, though never below the worst-case $\Omega(n^{1/3})$.

\paragraph{Polylogarithmic factors and determinism.}
\Cref{cor:tight} leaves a polylogarithmic and $\eps$-dependent gap. Pinning down the exact dependence on
$\eps$ and $\log\Delta$, and asking whether a deterministic range-counting algorithm can match the
$n^{1/3}$ rate or whether randomization is necessary at this oracle, are concrete questions about the
constant in the exponent's neighborhood. The empty-cell leader estimator is fundamentally randomized (it
relies on uniform ranks and spatial sampling), so a deterministic $n^{1/3}$ algorithm, if one exists,
would likely need a different idea for the component-counting step.
