\section{The clipped death-time primitive and the reduction for \texorpdfstring{$P$}{P}}\label{sec:dt}

\Cref{lem:support} estimates the MST weight of a grid support. To estimate $W=\wt(\MST(P))$ itself we
split the MST weight at a clip level $L$ into a \emph{bulk} part (edges up to $L$) and a \emph{tail} part
(the excess of long edges), estimate the bulk directly on $P$ by a death-time sampler, and route the tail
through a snapped support handled by \cref{lem:support}. This section states the two ingredients; the
reduction proofs are in \cref{sec:app-assembly}, and the WSPD implementation of the spanner in
\cref{sec:wspd}.

\subsection{The clipped death-time sampler}\label{sec:dt-sampler}

The support estimator integrates the cluster-count curve directly. For $P$ itself we instead sample the
MST edge weights through a device that turns ``how heavy is the MST'' into ``how far must a random vertex
travel to reach someone more senior,'' which a spanner lets us answer with a local search. The staircase
picture is \cref{fig:deathtime}: $c_S(t)-1$ counts the MST edges heavier than $t$, its area is the MST
weight, and the death times read that staircase off one vertex at a time.

Let $H$ be a connected weighted graph on $V$ with a fixed root $r$. Assign independent uniform ranks to
$V\setminus\{r\}$ and define the \emph{death time} $\tau(v)$ as the bottleneck (min--max-edge) distance
from $v$ to the root or to any lower-ranked vertex: the smallest $b$ such that $v$ can reach the root or a
lower-ranked vertex by a path of edges all $\le b$ (\cref{fig:dtsearch}). The name is apt: as the threshold $b$ grows from $0$,
$v$'s cluster keeps absorbing neighbors until, at $b=\tau(v)$, it first touches someone more senior and
$v$ ceases to be the leader of its own cluster. The death times recover the MST edge weights as a
multiset:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:death-multiset}
  \set{\tau(v):v\in V}=\set 0\cup\set{\wt(e):e\in\MST(H)},
\end{equation}
because for every $t$ the count $\abs{\{v:\tau(v)>t\}}$ equals $c_H(t)-1$, the number of MST edges heavier
than $t$ (a vertex survives past $t$ exactly when it is the minimum-rank vertex of a non-root cluster at
scale $t$, and there is one such vertex per non-root cluster). A bottleneck Dijkstra search from $v$ that
halts at the first lower-ranked vertex computes $\tau(v)$; because the search stops as soon as it meets
someone of smaller rank, and ranks are uniform, it extracts only $O(\log\abs V)$ vertices in expectation
(the $j$-th closest vertex is reached only if $v$ outranks the previous $j-1$, probability $\le 1/(j-1)$),
which is what makes a single death time cheap to compute. For a clip level $L$ set
$X_L(v)=\min\{\tau(v),L\}$. Integrating the tail identity up to $L$ gives $\abs V\cdot\E
X_L=A_L(H)\eqdef\sum_{e\in\MST(H)}\min\{\wt(e),L\}$, and $0\le X_L\le L$ forces $X_L^2\le L X_L$, so the
average of $s$ independent samples has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dt-var}
  \Var\Bigl[\tfrac{\abs V}{s}\textstyle\sum_i X_L(v_i)\Bigr]\le\frac{\abs V\,L\,\wt(\MST(H))}{s}.
\end{equation}
We apply this to a locally navigable Euclidean $(1+\rho)$-spanner $H_X$ of a point set $X$, realized under
the oracle by a WSPD (\cref{sec:wspd}). Since $H_X$ is a Euclidean subgraph and a $(1+\rho)$-spanner,
$c_X(t)\le c_{H_X}(t)\le c_X(t/(1+\rho))$, so integrating,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spanner-stretch}
  A_L(X)\le A_L(H_X)\le(1+\rho)\,A_L(X),
\end{equation}
i.e.\ the spanner inflates the clipped weight by at most $\rho\,\wt(\MST(X))$.

The death-time sampler handles the bulk of $P$, but its variance \eqref{eq:dt-var} grows with the clip
level, so it is efficient only up to $L$; the long edges beyond $L$ are rare and heavy, exactly the regime
the support estimator was built for. To hand them over we need a support whose size is the balance point
$K_0=\Theta(n^{2/3})$ and whose snapping error is controlled. Choosing the grid scale is the one place a
search is needed, because the right scale depends on how the (unseen) points are spread.

Fix a guess $G\ge W$, put $K_0=\ceil{n^{2/3}}$ and $L=G/K_0$. We choose a grid scale $h$ whose support
$Q$ has $K=\Theta(K_0)$ cells. How $K_h$, the number of nonempty $h$-cells, varies with $h$ is governed by
the same packing principle as the active cover: occupied cells of side $h$ are pairwise $\ge h$ apart in
each of four color classes, so their count is tied to the MST weight, giving a bound analogous to
\cref{lem:packing},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kh-packing}
  K_h\le\frac{8W}{h}+4 .
\end{equation}
This bound is one-directional ($K_h$ may be much smaller than $8W/h$ if the points cluster), which is
exactly why a search over $h$ is needed: the packing tells us $K_h$ cannot be too large at a given scale,
but only by trying scales can we find one where it is large enough, namely $\Theta(K_0)$.
Starting from a dyadic quadtree scale $h_0=\Theta(\xi L)$ (the largest dyadic side below $\xi L$, so $h_0$
is within a factor $2$ of $\xi L$) and halving until $K_h$ first reaches a constant multiple of $K_0$
(which is certified by a fixed batch of rejection trials, since halving $h$ at most quadruples $K_h$, and
halving keeps every scale dyadic) yields a scale with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K-window}
  cK_0\le K\le C_\xi K_0 ,
\end{equation}
in $\Otil_\xi(n/K_0)=\Otil_\xi(n^{1/3})$ queries; \cref{lem:kwindow} proves both the window and the cost.
Fix this $h$ and its support $Q$. Two degenerate scales are handled directly: if $\xi L<1$ then, since
$L=G/K_0\ge(n-1)/n^{2/3}=\Omega(n^{1/3})$, we have $n=O(\xi^{-3})=O_\eps(1)$, and the entire MST is computed
exactly by reading all $n$ points in $O_\eps(1)$ queries; and if $\xi L>\Delta$ the search starts at the
bounding-square scale (one cell, $K_h=1<cK_0$) and halves from there.

\subsection{Snapping}\label{sec:dt-snap}

Snapping replaces $P$ by the multiset $Q$ of its occupied-cell centers, which is what the support estimator
sees. The price is a small distortion of all distances, and the whole point of choosing $h=O(\xi L)$ is to
keep that distortion below the clip level so it perturbs only the tail, and only by an $O(\xi)$ fraction.
Snapping each point of $P$ to its $h$-cell center moves it by at most $\sqrt2\,h/2$ (the half-diagonal of
an $h$-cell), so any pairwise distance changes by at most $\delta_s=\sqrt2\,h$. A distance shift of at most
$\delta_s$ means any two points joined at threshold $t$ before snapping are joined at $t+\delta_s$ after,
and conversely, so the component counts interleave with a $\delta_s$ shift in the threshold:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:snap-interleave}
  c_P(t+\delta_s)\le\cQ(t)\le c_P(t-\delta_s)\qquad(t\ge\delta_s).
\end{equation}
At thresholds $t\ge L\ge 2\delta_s$ this shift is a relative $O(\delta_s/L)=O(\xi)$ perturbation, which is
why the snapping is harmless for the tail (the regime $t\ge L$) while it would be ruinous for the bulk (the
regime $t=\Theta(1)\ll L$) -- exactly the regime the death-time sampler keeps on $P$, never on $Q$.
Writing $B_L(X)=\int_L^\infty(c_X(t)-1)\,dt=\sum_{e\in\MST(X)}(\abs e-L)_+$ for the tail weight,
\eqref{eq:snap-interleave} together with $c_P(t)-1\le W/t$ gives, for $\delta_s\le L/2$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tail-transfer}
  \abs{B_L(Q)-B_L(P)}\le\int_{L-\delta_s}^{L+\delta_s}\frac Wt\,dt\le\frac{4\delta_s}{L}W=O(\xi W),
\end{equation}
by $\delta_s=\sqrt2\,h=O(\xi L)$ (\cref{lem:kwindow} gives $h\le h_0=\Theta(\xi L)$). Finally, contracting $\MST(P)$ onto the $h$-cells and using \eqref{eq:Kh-packing}
with $K=\Omega(K_0)$ gives $\Wq\le W+\delta_s(K-1)\le C_W W$ for an absolute constant $C_W$.

\subsection{The decomposition}\label{sec:dt-decomp}

We can now state precisely which three quantities the algorithm estimates and how they combine. The
guiding identity is that the MST weight splits as bulk plus tail at the clip level, and that the tail of
$P$ equals the tail of the snapped support up to the transfer error \eqref{eq:tail-transfer}; the support
tail in turn is its total weight minus its own bulk, both of which we can estimate. The reason for this
three-way split is that no single one of our two estimators handles both ends of the edge-length spectrum:
the death-time sampler is accurate only up to the clip $L$ (its variance grows with $L$), and the support
estimator sees only the snapped support, not $P$. Splitting at $L$ lets each estimator work in its own
regime and the snapping bridges the gap, at the cost of one extra death-time estimate on the support to
subtract off the bulk that the support and $P$ have in common.

Write $A_L(X)=\sum_{e\in\MST(X)}\min\{\abs e,L\}$, so $\wt(\MST(X))=A_L(X)+B_L(X)$. We use the bulk of $P$
and the tail of the snapped support:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp}
  W=A_L(P)+B_L(P),\qquad B_L(Q)=\Wq-A_L(Q),
\end{equation}
and transfer the tail by \eqref{eq:tail-transfer}. The three estimable pieces are $A_L(P)$ (death-time
sampler on $P$), $\Wq$ (\cref{lem:support}), and $A_L(Q)$ (death-time sampler on $Q$); we assemble them in
\cref{sec:main}.
